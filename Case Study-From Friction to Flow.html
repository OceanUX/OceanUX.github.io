Improving Ticket Creation and Tracking Across Roles

  • Role: UX Designer
  • Timeline: 2+ years
  • Team: 1 Product Manage, 2-4 Engineers (depending on the iteration)
  • Tools: Figma, FigJam, Jira

Problem Overview

Before the redesign, the ticketing process was entirely manual and fragmented.

Key pain points

  • No ticket visibility for customers
  • Time-consuming manual work for first-line and triage staff
  • High risk of miscommunication
  • Difficult to scale and manage

My Responsibility and Timeline

"My role covered end-to-end UX design, from research to post-launch support."

Decision Point

  • Submit a support incident ticket
  • Receive confirmation

Protoype - Ticket Creation

Select request type → Complete the form → Submit → Access ticket details

What I did

  • Designed a dynamic ticket form with conditionally displayed fields to keep things relevant
  • Added tooltips and smart defaults to guide users
  • Implemented an instant confirmation state with auto-generated ticket number and a clear call- to-action to view ticket details

Impact

  • Tickets became more consistent and complete, reducing manual clarification for the support team.
  • Users received immediate reassurance and visibility into their request without extra steps.

Design System Foundations

I developed a modular design system to support consistency and scalability.

This included accessible, reusable components such as form fields, status indicators, and layout patterns.

Defining these foundations early streamlined the design process, improved cross- functional alignment, and minimised rework during development.

Accessibility Compliance & Guided Onboarding

Accessibility Audit

  • Conducted a full accessibility review (WCAG 2.2)
  • Created a report with dev-friendly recommendations
  • Improved screen reader support, contrast, keyboard navigation, and labels

User Guidance Materials

To support adoption, I also created user-facing tutorials and internal documentation.

This included step-by-step guides, annotated screenshots, and training videos to help both end users and internal staff navigate the new system efficiently.

Iteration Steps: Improving the Triage Process

Step 1: Refined the categorisation matrix — including request type, service area, urgency level, and assignment group.

Step 2: Implemented pre-categorisation logic during ticket submission to guide users and improve accuracy.

Step 3: Aligned form structure with third- party tools to enable automatic routing to the appropriate internal team.

Efficiency Through UX Improvements

For Customer

  • Let customers help themselves without waiting on our team
  • Significantly reduced triage delays — customers no longer had to wait a full day for ticket assignment.

For Support Team

  • Enabled automated ticket routing, eliminating the need for 1–2 full-time triage roles.
  • Freed up internal teams to focus on resolving issues rather than managing ticket distribution.

Final Reflection & Impact

This project transformed an outdated, high-friction support model into a scalable, self-service platform that served customers and internal teams.

Outcomes

  • Successfully launched a self-service ticket creation and tracking system
  • Reduced triage time and manual workload by introducing auto-routing logic
  • Freed up 1–2 full-time triage roles, improving team efficiency
  • Customer wait time for ticket assignment dropped from 1 day to nearly instant
  • Ticket quality improved through smarter form structure and pre-categorisation
  • Supported future scalability with structured, reusable workflows

Key Challenges

  • Limited existing workflow documentation created ambiguity early on
  • Initial ticket submissions lacked structure, requiring smarter form logic
  • Triage rules across teams were inconsistent and undocumented
  • Had to balance simplicity for end users with complexity needed by internal teams
  • Educating users (internal and external) on the new system took significant effort
